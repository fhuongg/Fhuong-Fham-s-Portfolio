The work explores memory, dread, and the impossibility of saving Laura Palmer from Twin Peaks. This project stages repetition, memory, and disorientation as an endless loop. It asks: what if thresholds were not crossed, but inhabited?
Inspired by the keyword "liminality" and David Lynch's ability to evoke these dreadful feelings, the project uses looping rooms, unsettling audio, and interactive elements to immerse users in an eerie experience. Throughout the experience rooms are emerged with glitches and digital distortion.

Moodboard and research

The style combines hand-drawn imagery and digital distortion to create an unsettling hybrid environment, one that is both human and artificial.
Each room was hand-drawn in pencil and oil pastel texture, then composited in p5.js. This allowed for small imperfections to bleed into the digital environment, giving it an intimate, haunting feel.

Initial sketches and hand-drawn elements

The narrative unfolds through interaction. Each room introduces a different mechanic that reflects the emotional tone of liminality. Movement and stillness conflicts, and a second self is introduced.

The iconic red room is a liminal waiting space with cryptic dialogue options. Each choice leads to a memory scene (diner, forest, etc.), where the user performs small actions that destabilize the space.

The piece loops endlessly, reinforcing the feeling that escape is an illusion.

As this was my first large p5 project, I learned to manage complex logic while experimenting with visual instability.
Bringing Twin Peaks into a creative context was a personal milestone — a way of translating fascination into something I could call my own.
